My advisor suggested two possible methods: video analysis and IMU-based motion data acquisition.

Therefore, I decided to use IMUs to collect swimming motion data.

Design Concept of a Wearable IMU-Based System for Real-Time Feedback on Swimming Rhythm

Studying the rhythm of swimming is a fascinating topic that integrates technology, physiology, and data science. Swimming rhythm is not merely about ‚Äúhow fast you stroke‚Äù; it represents the dynamic balance between stroke frequency and stroke length (distance per stroke) within one full swimming cycle, typically quantified by stroke rate and stroke count.

Below is a complete methodology for studying swimming rhythm‚Äîfrom simple to complex, from qualitative to quantitative.

1. Defining Research Metrics: What to Measure?

Core Metrics

  • Stroke Rate (SR): Number of stroke cycles per unit time (strokes per minute).
    Example: In a 25 m pool, time = 20 s, strokes = 20 ‚Üí
    SR = 20 √∑ (20/60) = 60 strokes/min.
  • Stroke Count (SC): Number of strokes required to complete a fixed distance (e.g., 50 m).
    Also referred to as ‚Äústroke efficiency index.‚Äù
  • Rhythm: Sometimes refers to the distribution of time across phases within one arm stroke (e.g., pull, push, recovery).
    For example, the push phase may take up 40% of one cycle.

Derived Metrics

  • Swimming Velocity (V): Distance per unit time (m/s).
    Formula: V = Stroke Rate √ó Stroke Length
  • Stroke Length (SL): Average distance traveled per stroke (m/stroke).
    Formula: SL = Velocity √∑ Stroke Rate
    SL serves as a key indicator of efficiency and technique.

Associated Metrics

  • Heart Rate, Blood Lactate: Used to evaluate physiological load under a given rhythm.
  • Turn Time: Critical when analyzing rhythm across entire laps.

2. Data Acquisition Methods: How to Collect Data?

1) Manual Counting and Video Analysis (Low-Cost, High Value)

  • Method: Use a stopwatch and a high-frame-rate camera to record swimming.
  • Processing:
    • Manual counting of strokes and time to compute stroke rate and length.
    • Video software (e.g., Dartfish, Kinovea) can measure stroke phase durations and generate motion trajectories.
  • Pros: Simple, intuitive, low cost.
  • Cons: Time-consuming, not suitable for long-term continuous monitoring, limited data.

2) Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) ‚Äî The Core Tool of Modern Research

  • Sensor Placement: On wrists, upper back/lower torso, ankles, etc.

  • Data Collected:

    • Acceleration & Angular Velocity precisely capture strokes and kicks.

  • Data Analysis:

    • Automatic Event Detection: By analyzing wrist IMU acceleration, it‚Äôs possible to detect each stroke‚Äôs start and end, calculating stroke rate and phase durations.
    • Body Posture Analysis: Back-mounted IMU monitors body roll and stability.
    • Kicking Rhythm: Ankle IMU tracks kicking frequency and amplitude.
    • Turn Detection: Sudden changes in IMU data reveal turns and their timing.

  • Pros: Rich, objective, long-term continuous data; reveals subtle, imperceptible motion changes.

  • Cons: Requires signal processing and algorithm knowledge; devices add cost.

3) Pressure Sensors and Underwater Cameras

Used for in-depth biomechanical analysis of hand pressure and underwater trajectories‚Äîmostly in lab settings.

3. Research Path and Key Questions

The essence of rhythm research is studying the relationship between stroke rate and stroke length, and finding optimal patterns for different goals.

1) Building Individual ‚ÄúSpeed‚ÄìStroke Rate‚ÄìStroke Length‚Äù Curves

  • Method: Swim fixed distances at varying speeds, record time and stroke count, compute SR and SL.
  • Analysis: Plot SR‚ÄìSL curves. Typically, as speed increases, SR rises while SL first remains stable or increases slightly, then declines.
  • Goal: Identify the stroke rate where stroke length is maximized‚Äîoften the point of optimal efficiency.

2) Analyzing the Effect of Fatigue on Rhythm

  • Method: Monitor rhythm metrics continuously during long or intense training.
  • Analysis: Observe how SR and SL change as fatigue accumulates.
    • Common pattern: SR remains constant or increases, SL decreases ‚Äî indicating technical breakdown and efficiency loss.
    • Significance: Evaluates endurance and technical stability; informs training design.

3) Comparing Rhythms Between Different Technique Levels

  • Method: Compare data between elite and amateur swimmers.
  • Analysis:
    • Elites usually maintain longer SL and lower SR at the same speed.
    • Their rhythm stability is also higher.

4) Exploring Personalized Optimal Rhythms

  • Core Idea: There is no universal ‚Äúperfect rhythm.‚Äù Optimal rhythm depends on body structure, strength, flexibility, and technique.
  • Method: Combine physiological metrics (heart rate, VO‚ÇÇ) to find rhythm combinations with lowest energy cost and highest efficiency at a target intensity (e.g., lactate threshold).

4. Practical Application Case

Scenario: Helping an amateur swimmer improve 1500 m freestyle performance.

  1. Baseline Evaluation:
    Record with IMU or video. Observation: stroke count rises sharply in later laps; rhythm becomes unstable.
  2. Problem Diagnosis:
    Fatigue causes shorter stroke length; swimmer compensates by increasing stroke rate, breaking rhythm and reducing efficiency.
  3. Training Intervention:
    • Technical Training: Kickboard drills, single-arm swimming ‚Äî focus on improving stroke length.
    • Rhythm Training: Use a waterproof metronome (beep pace) to train at specific stroke rates, gradually adapting to higher tempos.
    • Endurance Training: High-intensity interval sessions while monitoring rhythm stability.
  4. Outcome Evaluation:
    Re-test after training; expect more stable stroke count, longer SL, improved overall time.

5. Summary

The study of swimming rhythm has evolved from traditional coaching intuition to a precision, data-driven discipline.

  • Core Concept: Understanding and optimizing Velocity = Stroke Rate √ó Stroke Length.
  • Tools: From stopwatch and camera to IMU sensors, tool choice defines data depth and accuracy.
  • Research Path: Build personal curves, analyze fatigue effects, perform comparisons, and find the athlete‚Äôs most efficient personalized rhythm.

Whether you are an athlete, coach, or researcher, mastering this framework brings a deeper understanding of swimming‚Äôs underlying mechanics.

When Engineering Meets Swimming: Turning Technique into Data

Background

Last year, I participated in a science and innovation competition at school. Under the guidance of my mentor, I worked on a drone project and became deeply involved in it. Through this project, I learned knowledge and skills related to control, mechanics, circuits, signal processing, motor driving, and sensor data transmission. My hands-on abilities improved significantly, and I developed a strong interest in creating technological works. The greatest sense of achievement came from seeing a device I designed, built, and debugged actually operate and perform tasks. Although the quadcopter project did not qualify in that competition, my mother reminded me that I had been practicing swimming since childhood and participated in many swimming competitions. Based on that background, she suggested that I could conduct an engineering research project combining swimming and technology. That idea excited me greatly, so I began research, design, and experiments related to it last year. I will record my experiences from the past year one by one in this blog.

Motivation: How to Improve My Swimming Performance

The purpose of doing research around my swimming training and competitions is, of course, to improve my swimming performance.
During daily training, my coach often emphasizes key points of swimming technique. How could I quickly summarize these technical essentials? I asked an AI: To improve my performance, I must first understand what affects it. The AI gave me the following answer.

Three Core Factors for Improving Swimming Performance

The AI summarized it as follows:
Improving swimming performance is a systematic process that can be attributed to three core elements: biomechanics (technique), physiology (physical ability), and psychology (mental strength). Among them, biomechanics (technique) is the most complex, delicate, and also the most suitable part to be analyzed and optimized using engineering methods.

1. Biomechanics: The ‚ÄúTechnical Soul‚Äù of Swimming

This part focuses on how to make the human body as a ‚Äúbiological machine‚Äù move through the water with maximum efficiency and minimal resistance.

  1. Reducing Resistance ‚Äì ‚ÄúBecoming Streamlined‚Äù

    • Streamlined body position: The foundation of all technique. The key is to keep the body as straight as possible at all times to minimize the frontal area. This includes:
      • Core stability: If your midsection (waist and abdomen) is unstable, it‚Äôs like a soft rope ‚Äî no matter how hard your limbs work, power is wasted and body wobble increases drag.
      • Head position: Eyes should look toward the bottom of the pool, keeping the spine aligned, as if a string is pulling your head forward.
      • Body rotation: Especially in freestyle and backstroke, moderate rotation around the spine reduces shoulder entry resistance and better engages core strength.

  2. Increasing Propulsion ‚Äì ‚ÄúPulling Efficiently‚Äù

    • High-elbow catch: The core source of propulsion. You don‚Äôt push water with your palms; instead, you ‚Äúanchor‚Äù your arms and hands in the water and pull your body past that fixed point using your torso and shoulders. Early vertical forearm is the key to achieving a high-elbow catch.
    • Power sequence and core activation: The pulling motion should not rely solely on the arms. The correct order is ‚Äúcore initiates ‚Üí transfers to shoulders ‚Üí then to arms,‚Äù like a whip motion. Your core muscles are the engine, while the arms are just the drive shafts.
    • Surface area and feel for water: You should use not just your palms but also your forearms as a single surface during the catch phase ‚Äî this is known as ‚Äúfeel for the water.‚Äù

  3. Rhythm and Timing ‚Äì ‚ÄúMaking Movements Harmonious‚Äù

    • Breathing timing: Breathing is the only movement that disrupts the streamlined body position and rhythm. It must be completed quickly and lightly during the trough of body rotation to minimize interference.
    • Coordination of arms and legs: The main role of kicking is to balance the body, lift the lower limbs, provide partial propulsion, and assist the core. The rhythm of the kick must be precisely synchronized with the stroke.

2. Physiology: The ‚ÄúEnergy Engine‚Äù of Swimming

Without physical conditioning, even the best technique is meaningless.

  1. Aerobic capacity: The foundation for long-distance events, determining sustained power output and recovery speed.
  2. Anaerobic capacity: Determines sprint ability and acceleration after starts and turns.
  3. Strength and flexibility:
    • Dryland strength: Especially core, back (main pulling muscles), and shoulder strength.
    • In-water strength: Specific strength for generating force in a water environment.
    • Flexibility: Excellent shoulder and ankle flexibility (particularly plantar flexion) directly increase stroke length and kick efficiency.

3. Psychology: The ‚ÄúCommander‚Äù of Swimming

  1. Feel for water: An intuition of how water flows and how to ‚Äúgrip‚Äù it ‚Äî developed through thousands of strokes.
  2. Rhythm and focus: Maintaining form under fatigue, focusing on personal rhythm and tactics without being disturbed by competitors.
  3. Tactical intelligence: Includes energy distribution, start and turn precision, and situational awareness during races.

How Can These Elements Be Combined with My Engineering Project? ‚Äî Entry Points from Engineering Perspective

After learning the three key elements of swimming performance, I wanted to find a feasible research topic. Since I still lack a lot of professional engineering knowledge, I hope the research goal can be small and achievable. Here are the AI‚Äôs suggestions:
Use engineering tools to quantify, analyze, and optimize the above biomechanical elements.

  • For ‚ÄúReducing Resistance‚Äù:

    • Engineering research: You can design an underwater video analysis system (using waterproof sports cameras) combined with computer vision algorithms to automatically identify and quantify body posture angles (e.g., hip drop angle, head tilt) and compute cross-sectional area changes ‚Äî much more precise than a coach‚Äôs visual observation.

  • For ‚ÄúIncreasing Propulsion‚Äù:

    • Engineering research: You could develop a wearable sensor system (IMUs placed on wrists, elbows, and shoulders) to measure high-elbow angles, stroke path stability, and stroke frequency. You might also build a simple hand pressure sensor to quantify ‚Äúfeel for water,‚Äù i.e., how much force the swimmer applies during the catch phase.

  • For ‚ÄúRhythm and Timing‚Äù:

    • Engineering research: Using the same IMU or video analysis, you could automatically calculate stroke cycle duration, breathing side rotation angle, and kick-stroke synchronization, generating visualized data reports.

In summary:
Your unique advantage is that you not only know these key elements ‚Äî you have felt them with your body. Now your task is to use engineering technology to translate those ‚Äúfeelings‚Äù into ‚Äúdata,‚Äù turning the coach‚Äôs ‚Äúexperience‚Äù into ‚Äúscientific evidence.‚Äù

After reviewing these suggestions, I realized that the ‚Äúrhythm and timing‚Äù category might be the easiest to implement. It reminds me of PWM waveforms in motor control ‚Äî alternating between high and low states. I could use similar methods to find such cycles. And indeed, swimming rhythm is crucial. From this, I found my research direction.
