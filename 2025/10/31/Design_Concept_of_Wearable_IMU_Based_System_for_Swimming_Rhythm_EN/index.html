  • Rhythm: Sometimes refers to the distribution of time across phases within one arm stroke (e.g., pull, push, recovery).

2. Data Acquisition Methods: How to Collect Data?

1) Manual Counting and Video Analysis (Low-Cost, High Value)

  • Method: Use a stopwatch and a high-frame-rate camera to record swimming.
  • Processing:
    • Manual counting of strokes and time to compute stroke rate and length.
    • Video software (e.g., Dartfish, Kinovea) can measure stroke phase durations and generate motion trajectories.
  • Pros: Simple, intuitive, low cost.
  • Cons: Time-consuming, not suitable for long-term continuous monitoring, limited data.

2) Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) ‚Äî The Core Tool of Modern Research

  • Sensor Placement: On wrists, upper back/lower torso, ankles, etc.

  • Data Collected:

    • Acceleration & Angular Velocity precisely capture strokes and kicks.

  • Data Analysis:

    • Automatic Event Detection: By analyzing wrist IMU acceleration, it‚Äôs possible to detect each stroke‚Äôs start and end, calculating stroke rate and phase durations.
    • Body Posture Analysis: Back-mounted IMU monitors body roll and stability.

    • Turn Detection: Sudden changes in IMU data reveal turns and their timing.

  • Pros: Rich, objective, long-term continuous data; reveals subtle, imperceptible motion changes.

  • Cons: Requires signal processing and algorithm knowledge; devices add cost.

3. Research Path and Key Questions

The essence of rhythm research is studying the relationship between stroke rate and stroke length, and finding optimal patterns for different goals.

1) Building Individual ‚ÄúSpeed‚ÄìStroke Rate‚ÄìStroke Length‚Äù Curves

  • Method: Swim fixed distances at varying speeds, record time and stroke count, compute SR and SL.
  • Analysis: Plot SR‚ÄìSL curves. Typically, as speed increases, SR rises while SL first remains stable or increases slightly, then declines.
  • Goal: Identify the stroke rate where stroke length is maximized‚Äîoften the point of optimal efficiency.

2) Analyzing the Effect of Fatigue on Rhythm

  • Method: Monitor rhythm metrics continuously during long or intense training.
  • Analysis: Observe how SR and SL change as fatigue accumulates.
    • Common pattern: SR remains constant or increases, SL decreases ‚Äî indicating technical breakdown and efficiency loss.
    • Significance: Evaluates endurance and technical stability; informs training design.

3) Comparing Rhythms Between Different Technique Levels

  • Method: Compare data between elite and amateur swimmers.
  • Analysis:
    • Elites usually maintain longer SL and lower SR at the same speed.

  • Core Idea: There is no universal ‚Äúperfect rhythm.‚Äù Optimal rhythm depends on body structure, strength, flexibility, and technique.
